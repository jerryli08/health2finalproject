Don't Ball Up Your Stress

How procrastination and social media are destroying our mental health, and what to do about it

Academic Stress Is at a Breaking Point

Stress due to academics is at an all-time high for high-schoolers. A report by the American Psychological Association, the APA, found that the primary cause of stress for 70% of teenagers is academic stress. Any kind of stress, including academic stress, can often cause anxiety and sleep deprivation.

Procrastination is when a student decides themselves to delay a task even though they know that doing so will only compress their timeline to complete the task and increase their stress. Many students procrastinate to the point it affects their grades and wellbeing, usually because they are trying to avoid the negative emotions tied to difficult schoolwork.

What Stress Does to Your Brain

When you are stressed, your brain floods your body with cortisol. Cortisol is the "stress hormone". While short bursts of it help you stay alert, chronic stress keeps your cortisol levels elevated for too long. Research shows that extended exposure to cortisol can actually shrink your hippocampus, the part of the brain responsible for learning and memory, while weakening the connections in your prefrontal cortex, which makes it hard to concentrate or make good decisions. (Baptist Health)

Your Phone Is Designed to Steal Your Focus

Social media is designed specifically to grab your attention and keep you unfocused, using the same tricks used by slot machines at casinos. App developers design these platforms using "variable ratio reinforcement schedules," which is the exact same psychological trick that slot machines use to keep people gambling. (Neurosity Guide) Since we know gambling is highly addictive and we also know that social media uses the same tricks, we should treat social media with the same level of rigor.

How Dopamine Can Trap You

Dopamine is the chemical behind why ever notification, new post, or like you feels good. Dopamine is a chemical neurotransmitter in your brain, and neuroscientists found through research that dopamine doesn't release when you feel pleasure, but instead during the anticipation of a reward. So instead of getting addicted to the "pleasure," your brain gets addicted to the feeling in anticipation of checking your phone, as it is unpredictable if there will be a new notification or not. This causes a never-ending cycle that fragments your attention span, as it is very difficult to stop this behavior. (Neurosity Guide)

Anxiety due to Social Media and Toxic Self-Comparisons

One of the biggest problems with social media is that it causes you to compare your life to others. Comparing your life to other people increases your anxiety and lowers your self-esteem, as you are constantly compared to unrealistic standards, whether that is people who are best-of-the-best, or people who fabricate things about themselves. The American Psychological Association indicates that around 60% of teens directly say that social media is a cause of their stresses.

5 Ways to Stop the Cycle of Academic Stress

Each strategy below has been tested in academic research on teenage students.

Daily Time Blocking (Time Management)

Instead of just making a to-do list, you should schedule out specific blocks of time for your tasks. You could separate blocks for your homework, and also include breaks and social media in a controlled manner. Research shows that scheduled windows for breaks and distracting apps reduces the brain's urge to want to switch tasks and do the easier task, helping you keep be focused. (Leroy, Sophie) I personally use Google Calendar to plan out my tasks every day.

Conditional Planning (Behavioral Psychology)

You can use "conditional statements" to make your daily choices regarding tasks easier. For example you could say "If its 4 PM I'll open my textbook and start doing my homework." Psychology research from Fordham University prove that high schoolers who use conditional planning start their homework up to many hours faster just because it takes away the energy needed to make a conscious decision since the system has already decided for you.

Having a Study Environment Without a Phone (Digital Wellness)

By putting your phone in another room or having it completely turned off while you study, you can stay focused. This is proven with Duke University's Center for Advanced Hindsight's research which found that just disabling the notifications on your phone reduces the amount of times someone picks up their phone and does not want to 20%. This means that without your phone in the room, you don't have the notifications that cause you to want to pick up your phone as much.

Work in Short Sessions (Focus Technique)

You should break down your study time down into short, unbroken blocks, like 25 minutes of work followed by a 5-minute break, which is known as the Pomodoro Technique. Researchers found that being focused attention in short and predictable sections strengthens your brain's prefrontal cortex, the part of your brain responsible for decision-making and memory, and also increases your attention span. (Ogut, Eren)

Track Your Time Weekly (Self-Awareness)

Use a visual chart/tracker to log where your 24 hours actually go every day. This allows you to see a clear diagram of where your time goes every day, which allows you to be aware of your time in a more objective way. This makes it easier to determine what activities waste your time. (Svartdal, F., et al.)
